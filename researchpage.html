Excited State Proton Transfer with Chiral Molecules

Excited state proton transfer (ESPT) is a relatively new area of research that was first reported in the 1930s by Weber. By 1950s, the mechanism was defined by Forster. This mechanism has since adopted a critical role in lasers, bio-imaging, fluorescent sensors, energy storage devices, and other materials applications.

Up until recently, most research with ESPT focused on achiral molecules. The new interest in this field uses chiral dyes to facilitate ESPT for applications such as enantioselective sensing and asymmetric synthetic chemistry.

ESPT in Chiral Fluorescent Sensors

The most common use of ESPT to/from chiral molecules is in enantioselective fluorescent sensing. This is due to their large apparent Stokes shift which limits self-absorption and lets the excitation and detection wavelengths be easily distinguishable. Chiral ESPT dyes have been effective at detecting chiral amines, amino acids, carboxylic acids, and more using fluorescence and anisotropy measurements

Substituted BINOL

Since the initial report of BINOL based enantioselective sensors and substrates, the field has been greatly expanded. The derivatives of BINOL are typically symmetrically substituted. The main point of changing substitutents is to enhance sensitivity and enantioselectivity.
